The Number System

A universal language for notes, chords, and progressions.

Every key in music shares the same internal structure. The number system replaces note names with numbers, revealing the pattern that all keys have in common. Once you think in numbers, you can instantly transpose anything to any key. With every song you learn, you start recognising shapes that repeat everywhere.

There are two layers to the system: Nashville numbers for individual notes, and Roman numerals for chords.

Nashville Numbers: The Notes

Take any note as your root and call it 1. Every other note gets a number based on its distance from the root. The twelve notes of the chromatic scale become:

Number 1 b2 2 b3 3 4 #4/b5 5 b6 6 b7 Maj7
In C C Db D Eb E F F#/Gb G Ab A Bb B
In G G Ab A Bb B C C#/Db D Eb E F F#

The highlighted numbers (1 2 3 4 5 6 Maj7) are the seven notes of the major scale. The others are chromatic passing tones, labelled with flats or sharps relative to those scale degrees.

The power of this is immediate: a melody described as 1 3 5 6 5 is the same pattern whether the key is C, G, Bb, or anything else. The numbers are the permanent address; the note names are just a local translation.

Roman Numerals: The Chords

Chords built on each degree of the major scale get a Roman numeral. Uppercase means major; lowercase means minor. This is the natural chord map of any major key:

Numeral I ii iii IV V vi vii°
Quality Major minor minor Major Major minor dim
In C C Dm Em F G Am Bdim
In G G Am Bm C D Em F#dim
In A A Bm C#m D E F#m G#dim

These chords can all be modified, for example v would be a minor chord built on the fifth degree. Chords from outside the key use accidentals: bVII is a major chord built a whole step below the root. bIII is a major chord on the flattened third. Extensions attach directly: V7 is a dominant seventh, IVmaj7 is a major seventh on the fourth degree, Vsus4 is a suspended fourth on the fifth.
