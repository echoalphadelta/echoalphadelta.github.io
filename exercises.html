Exercises

These exercises build the muscle memory and fretboard knowledge that theory alone cannot give. Each one targets a specific weakness. Work slowly, then increase the tempo only when you can play it without thinking.

01 — Chord Transition Drill

Select a progression and set the tempo. The drill advances to the next chord automatically. The goal is to be ready before the change arrives — not to react to it.

Tempo 60 bpm · each chord lasts 4 beats

02 — Shape Anchor Drill

Exercise 02
Find the Root in Every Shape
All ShapesBeginner

Call out a random note — say, F#. Find the root of that note in the Echo shape first (low E string). Then find the Alpha root (A string). Then find the Delta root (D and B strings).

Do not move on to a new note until you can locate all three roots without hesitating. Work through all twelve notes of the chromatic scale. Use the Fretboard Trainer to test yourself under time pressure.

03 — One Shape, All Keys

Exercise 03
Travel the Neck in a Single Shape
EchoAlphaDeltaBeginner

Pick one shape — start with Echo. Play a major chord in that shape at every fret from 1 to 12, saying the chord name aloud as you play it. E at fret 1 becomes F. F becomes F#. Continue up the neck.

Then do the same for Alpha, starting on the A string. Then Delta on the D string. After completing all three shapes, you will have played every major chord in every position on the neck.

04 — Three Shapes, One Chord

Exercise 04
Play the Same Chord Three Ways
All ShapesIntermediate

Choose any chord — say, G major. Find it in the Echo shape. Play it. Without lifting your hand from the neck area, find it in the Delta shape. Then find it in the Alpha shape.

The three voicings should all be within a 4-fret range of each other on the neck at any given key. If you have to jump more than that, you are not moving between adjacent shapes — check your anchor frets.

05 — Box to Box

Exercise 05
Connect the Pentatonic Zones
All ShapesIntermediate

Play the minor pentatonic box pattern inside the Echo zone. When you reach the highest note, do not stop — find the same note in the Delta zone and continue the pentatonic run upward. From Delta, move into Alpha.

The goal is a single unbroken melodic line that moves through all three zones. This teaches you to treat the fretboard as one continuous surface rather than three isolated boxes. Practise both ascending and descending.

06 — Interval Targeting

Exercise 06
Find Every 3rd, 5th, and 7th
All ShapesIntermediate

Within a single shape, locate every instance of the 3rd interval. Play only those notes — no roots, no fifths. Listen to the character of the 3rd by itself. Then do the same for the 5th, then the 7th.

Knowing where each interval lives inside a shape means you can target chord tones while soloing. When the chord changes, you know which notes to lean on and which to pass through quickly.

07 — Slow Hands

Exercise 07
Play at Half the Speed You Think You Should
All ShapesAdvanced

Take any exercise above and set the tempo to the slowest setting you can tolerate. The target is perfect execution on every note — clean contact, full sustain, no buzz. Your brain cannot build reliable pathways from sloppy repetition at speed.

Increase the tempo by 5 bpm only when you can play through the entire exercise three consecutive times without a single error. This will feel slower than necessary. That is correct.
