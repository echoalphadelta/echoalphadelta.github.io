The Landscape of Sound

Every scale is a set of intervals — specific distances between notes. The major scale has seven. The pentatonic has five. The three shapes provide a way to see those notes on the fretboard without memorising individual fret-string positions one by one.

Shape Zones

Each shape occupies a stretch of the neck. Echo sits low, anchored by a root on the low E string. Delta sits in the middle, anchored on the D string. Alpha sits higher up, anchored on the A string. Together the three zones cover every note in any scale across all twelve frets. Change the key above and the zones shift together as a unit.

Connecting Zones

Scale notes at the edge of one zone sit next to the edge of the adjacent zone. This overlap is not accidental — the shapes are designed to meet. A player who can step from one zone into the next without losing their place has effectively learned the full neck. Start by finding the same root note in all three zones. Then find the 5 in each zone. Then the 3. Anchor points first, fill in the rest later.

The Pentatonic First

Start with five notes before seven. The pentatonic scale removes the 4 and the 7 from the major scale — the two notes with the most tension toward resolution. What remains works over almost any chord in almost any style. Each shape contains a natural pentatonic box. Learn the box, then add the two tension notes back to complete the full scale.

Minor and Modes

Every major key has a relative minor sharing the same notes. In the number system, the minor root sits on the 6 of the parent major. The shapes do not change — only what the ear decides is home. Modes work the same way: Dorian starts on the 2, Mixolydian starts on the 5. Shift your sense of root and the same three shapes become a different modal landscape.
